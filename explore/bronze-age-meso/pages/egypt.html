These tribes were commonly at conflict with eachother and agreed with very little. Eventually two promate powers emgried at the Nile, Upper Egypt and Lower Egypt.

Upper Egypt was based in Thebes while Lower Egypt was based in Memphis. Oddly engough, Upper Egypt was to the South of Lower Egypt. However, that would all change in 3150 BCE when King Narmer of Upper Egypt united the two kingdoms into one. Kingdom of Egypt, with Narmer as the first Pharaoh. Divine Right is a jusification for an autocratic rule, rule by one person. It goes as I just explained. A god or goddess has granted the right to rule in the hands of a person and therefor, is better prepared to rule. So when the ruler would die, then who rules the kingdom after? Well, if he was blessed, then his son would be blessed too, right? So the natural assumtion is that the son of the king would assume the throne as the old king died. If their was no son then the daughter, then the brothers, or sisters, or really any family member. This was called succession, who would succeed the king after he died. Now, succession was not always so easy. Sometimes there would be now close family members, or the only family member is cruel or incompetent, proving he might actually not be blessed by a god, so a powerful noble might try his luck to see if the rest of the court would support him becoming king, or in this case Pharaoh. And other nobles would support other nobles, causing a complicated succession. Usually these ended smoothly, but occasionally it didn't.

Sometimes no clear hier is in place, and many nobles all are completing over the throne. Occasionally it has came to violence. Pretenders would raise armies to try to press their claims as Pharaoh and take it by force. This may become too much for the government to handle, so it would fall apart. These periods were called "intermittent periods" and they seperated the 3 main Egyptian kingdoms, the Old Kingdom (the first), the Middle Kingdom (the second), and the New Kingdom (the last). The intermittent periods were periods of anarchy, with rival factions would try to claim and hold the throne of Egypt. These periods of lawlessness could be over a hundred years long, until finally a noble family beats the rest and wins the Egyptian throne.

Egyptian Conquests and Dipomacy

Egypt's New Kingdom was known for rapid conquests. The two main domains it asserted its control over was Nubia and Cannan. Cannan was notable at the time because it was not really Egyptian. Cannan was a kingdom in modern day Isreal/Palestine, well it actually was not a kingdom. Like how Egypt was ruled by local Chiefs before it was united by Narmer, Cannan the same way. It was a confederation of tribal states. They often fought with eachother but would band together to repeal an enemy. In this case the enemy was Ancient Egypt. In 1458 BCE, Pharaoh Thutmose III of Egypt defeated a coalition of local Cannanite Chiefs at the Battle of Megiddo. The confederation had lost their power and Cannan was soon overrun by Egypt.

Why did Egypt conquer Cannan? Primary because Cannan was a vastly imporatant area for trade, it gave Egypt many a safe rout to the rest of the Middle East, and better trade. But that was not only the reason, Thutmose III used Cannan as a message to the rest of the Mesopotomia: Don't Mess with Egypt. This move made Egypt a great power in Mesopotomia\ now it was no longer a isolated kingdom but a true empire that could conquer other civilizations.
